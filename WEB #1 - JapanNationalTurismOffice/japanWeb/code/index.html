A BIT OF OUR HISTORY

The origin of Japan

日本の起源

The history of Japan is the succession of events that occurred within the Japanese archipelago. Some of these events appear isolated and influenced by the geographical nature of Japan as anisland nation, while another series of events are due to foreign influences, as in the case of the Chinese Empire, which defined its language, its writing and, also, its political culture.


Likewise, another of the foreign influences was that of Western origin,which turned the country into an industrial nation, thereby exercising a sphere of influence and territorial expansion over the Pacific area. Throughout its long history, Japan has experienced periods of isolation and openness to world.

Contemporary Japan

現代日本

In contemporary Japan, the unique fusion of tradition and modernity it is evident in all aspects of daily life. The bustling cities, like Tokyo and Osaka, are centers of technological innovation and design avant-garde urban, while ancient temples and gardens historic buildings remain as tangible reminders of the rich heritage Culture of the country.


Japanese society, highly organized and disciplined, adapts to the demands of a globalized economy, highlighting the efficiency and dedication to work. At the same time, the pop culture phenomenon, from anime and manga to J-pop music, has transcended borders, becoming a global influence.

Jomon period (14.300 b.C - 300 a.C).-

The Jomon Period is a prehistoric period in the history of Japan that dates back to It extends from approximately 14,000 B.C. until around 300 BC. This period is known for the Jomon culture, which is the archaeological culture.


Oldest identified in the Japanese archipelago. Some features key to the Jomon Period are: Jomon pottery, hunting, fishing and gathering, society and housing, funerary rituals, stone tools, community development and agriculture.

Yayoi Period (300 a.C. - 300 d.C.).-

The Yayoi Period in Japanese history extends from approximately 300 BC. until 300 AD This period is crucial because it marks a major change in culture and technology in Japan.

Kofun Period (300 - 638).-

The Kofun Period in the history of Japan, which lasted from around 300 to 638 CE, is named after the distinctive keyhole-shaped burial mounds called "kofun." This era is characterized by the establishment of powerful clans, the emergence of a centralized political authority, and the construction of large burial mounds for elite individuals.

Asuka Period (638 - 710).-

The Asuka period is a crucial era in Japanese history that spans from 538 to 710 AD. Named after the Asuka region, where the imperial court was situated at the time, this period is marked by significant political, social, and cultural developments. The Asuka period is a subset of the larger Kofun period and is followed by the Nara period.

Nara Period (710 - 794).-

The Nara period, also known as the Nara era, is a significant period in Japanese history that spans from 710 to 794 AD. It follows the Asuka period and precedes the Heian period. The Nara period is characterized by political consolidation, cultural achievements, and the establishment of a centralized imperial government.

Heian Period (794 - 1186).-

The Heian period, which lasted from 794 to 1185 AD, is a significant era in Japanese history. It follows the Nara period and precedes the Kamakura period. Named after the capital city Heian-kyo, which is present-day Kyoto, this period is known for its flourishing arts and culture, as well as significant political and social developments.

Kamakura Period (1186 - 1333).-

The Kamakura period in Japanese history extends from 1185 to 1333 AD. It follows the Heian period and is named after the city of Kamakura, where Minamoto no Yoritomo established the Kamakura shogunate. This period is characterized by the rise of feudalism, the dominance of samurai warriors, and the establishment of a military government.

Muromachi Period (1333 - 1336).-

The Muromachi period, also known as the Ashikaga period, is a significant era in Japanese history that spans from 1336 to 1573 AD. It follows the Kamakura period and precedes the Azuchi-Momoyama period. The period is named after the district of Kyoto where the Ashikaga shogunate had its headquarters.

Muromachi Period (1336 - 1673).-

The Muromachi period, also known as the Ashikaga period, is a significant era in Japanese history that spans from 1336 to 1573 AD. It follows the Kamakura period and precedes the Azuchi-Momoyama period. The period is named after the district of Kyoto where the Ashikaga shogunate had its headquarters.

Azuchi-Momoyama Period (1673 - 1868).-

This era follows the Muromachi period and precedes the Edo period. The Azuchi-Momoyama period is characterized by significant political unification, military campaigns, and cultural developments. The period is named after two iconic locations: Azuchi Castle, built by Oda Nobunaga, and Momoyama, the region around Toyotomi Hideyoshi's castle.

Showa Period (1926 - 1989).-

The Shōwa period is a significant era in Japanese history that spans from December 25, 1926, to January 7, 1989. It encompasses the reign of Emperor Hirohito, the longest in Japanese history, and it is divided into two main phases: the pre-war period (1926-1945) and the post-war period (1945-1989).

Heisei Period (1868 - 1989).-

The Heisei era is a period in Japanese history that spanned from January 8, 1989, to April 30, 2019. It corresponds to the reign of Emperor Akihito. The Heisei era is characterized by significant social, economic, and technological changes, as well as notable events that shaped contemporary Japan.
